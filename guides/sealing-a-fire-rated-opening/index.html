Sealing a fire-rated opening

The door is rated — so why does the perimeter matter?

A fire door is not certified on its own. It is certified as an assembly — the leaf, the frame, the hardware that operates it and the components that close the gap around it, tested together in a specific configuration. The label on the leaf records the result of that test, not a property the leaf carries around independently.

This is the single most useful thing to understand about fire openings, because it explains almost everything that goes wrong with them on site.

The gap is the point

Fire spreads through openings largely as hot gas and smoke, and it finds the easiest path. On a door, that path is the perimeter: the clearance around the leaf, the meeting edges of a pair, the gap along the hinge stile, and the space under the leaf at the floor.

Those clearances are not accidental. A door needs them to swing, to clear the floor finish, and to accommodate the building moving and the leaf itself changing slightly with humidity. So the design problem is not “eliminate the gap” — it is close the gap under fire conditions while leaving the door able to operate normally the rest of the time.

That is what perimeter components do:

  • Intumescent seals stay unobtrusive at normal temperatures and expand when heated, filling the clearance and sealing the perimeter as the fire develops.
  • Smoke seals address the cold-smoke path — the part that endangers people well before the assembly is structurally threatened.
  • Gap and hinge-side protection deals with the hinge stile, which is both a fire path and, in occupied buildings, a genuine pinch hazard.
  • The threshold and bottom seal close the floor gap, which is often the largest single opening in the whole assembly.

Every one of those is a component someone has to specify, price and install. None of them are implied by the words “fire-rated door” on a schedule.

Why field modification is the real risk

Because the assembly is certified as tested, changes made on site can take it outside what was tested. The common ones look harmless in the moment:

  • Trimming the leaf to fix a fit problem or to clear new flooring.
  • Undercutting the door to help air movement between spaces.
  • Cutting in a lite or louver that was not part of the original prep.
  • Substituting hardware for whatever is in the van on the day.
  • Painting over or removing intumescent seals during a repaint.

Each of these can invalidate the label. And the label is what an inspector, an insurer and — eventually — an incident investigation will look for. A door that is physically similar to a listed assembly but no longer matches its listing is, formally, not a fire door.

The practical consequence for a project: decide the cut-outs, the hardware preps and the sealing package before the door is made, so the work happens at the factory under the listing rather than on site outside it. This is most of the argument for factory-prepped openings, and it is a schedule and labour argument as much as a compliance one.

What this means when you are specifying

A useful habit is to treat the perimeter as a line item rather than an assumption. When a schedule says “fire-rated door”, ask:

  1. What closes the gap at the head and jambs?
  2. What closes it at the floor — and does the floor finish change under this door?
  3. Is there smoke as well as fire to address on this opening?
  4. Is the hinge side protected, and is that a life-safety requirement here or a comfort one?
  5. Are the cut-outs and hardware preps all captured before fabrication?

None of that requires knowing a rating number. It requires knowing that the assembly is the unit of certification, and that the gap is where assemblies underperform.

Keep in mind
