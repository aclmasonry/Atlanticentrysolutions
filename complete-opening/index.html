The Performance Opening

An opening is an assembly, not a door.

Frame, leaf, glazing, louver, seals and threshold each come from a different factory — and each one decides how the finished opening performs. Select any component to see what it does and which line supplies it.

Glass, glazing kit and frame assembled as one unit and glazed into the leaf — the glass and the frame that holds it are certified together, not separately.

The clearest proof of the one-contact case: three lines coordinated into a single assembly, quoted and delivered together.

Perimeter seals

Close the gap around the leaf — head, jambs and meeting edges — controlling air, light and sound movement through the opening.

The gap is where openings underperform. Sealing it is the cheapest performance you can specify.

Openings that move

The sliding assembly.

Not every opening swings. A sliding leaf is carried overhead rather than hinged, which moves the engineering into the track and the pocket — and removes the threshold entirely. Select a component to see what it does and which line supplies it.

Select a component of the sliding assembly

Track & hangers

Carries the entire weight of the leaf and sets how it runs. The track is fixed to structure — not to the finish — and the hangers are matched to the leaf weight.

The part that decides whether the door still slides properly in five years. Under-specifying it is the most common reason a sliding opening gets warranty attention.

Supplied by KrisTrack

KrisTrack — track systems & hardware (opens manufacturer site in a new tab)

Sliding leaf

The same wood or hollow-metal construction used on a swing opening, prepped instead for hanger hardware and an edge pull rather than hinges and a lockset.

Wood or steel is still your choice here — sliding does not force a material, and the finish can match the swing doors around it.

Either line Lambton Door DeLa Fontaine

Lambton — architectural wood doors (opens manufacturer site in a new tab) DeLa Fontaine — hollow-metal doors (opens manufacturer site in a new tab)

Pocket frame

The concealed cavity the leaf slides into, framed so the wall stays straight and the door has a true path. Supplied knock-down, half knock-down or ready-made.

Buying the pocket as a manufactured frame is what stops a bowed wall becoming a door that binds — the failure that is effectively unfixable once the wall is closed.

Supplied by KrisTrack

KrisTrack — pocket door frames (opens manufacturer site in a new tab)

Floor guide

Keeps the bottom of the leaf in plane without carrying its weight. A sliding door has no threshold — this is what stops it swinging out of line.

Small, cheap, and the item most often left off a schedule. Its absence is what makes a new sliding door feel loose.

Supplied by KrisTrack

KrisTrack — track accessories & guides (opens manufacturer site in a new tab)

Why it matters

One assembly, one point of contact.

Coordinating six components across six factories is where commercial openings go wrong — a lite kit that doesn't suit the leaf, a threshold that doesn't suit the frame, a seal specified after the door was ordered. Representing complementary, non-competing lines means those decisions get made together, once.

For estimators, that means one call instead of six. For spec writers, it means the assembly is coherent before it reaches the project manual.
